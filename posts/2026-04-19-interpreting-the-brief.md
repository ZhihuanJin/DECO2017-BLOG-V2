At the beginning of this project, I interpreted the brief as asking for more than just “an app idea”. The main challenge is to identify what a specific community actually needs a web application to do, and then make design decisions around those needs. This means the early stage is not only about brainstorming features, but also about judging whether the idea has a clear purpose, a realistic scope, and enough difference from existing platforms.

Our initial ideas were quite broad. One possibility was a community for retro game enthusiasts. This could have included game collections, discussion boards, event listings, or recommendations. However, the more I considered it, the more it felt close to existing online communities such as Reddit, Discord, or specialist gaming forums. Another idea was a platform for anime fans, but this faced a similar issue: the audience is large, but the functional requirements are not very distinctive unless the app introduces a highly specific activity. A third possibility was a USyd “tree hole” style platform where students could anonymously post thoughts, problems, or confessions. This had emotional relevance, but it also raised moderation and safety concerns, especially around anonymous content, mental health, and harmful posts. It would also be difficult to manage responsibly within the scale of a prototype.

After comparing these options, the idea that currently seems strongest is a local community platform for people aged 55 and above, inspired by the neighbourhood logic of Nextdoor but adapted toward companionship and guided social connection. Importantly, I do not want to frame this as a dating app. Dating apps often focus on attraction, profile browsing, and one-to-one matching. Our concept is more about companionship, local belonging, and activity-first social interaction. The goal is to help older adults find people nearby to walk with, attend small activities with, share interests with, or slowly build trust through guided introductions.

This direction seems more appropriate for the brief because it has clearer functional requirements. The application would need a way for users to enter or select their local community area, browse nearby activities or social opportunities, express interest in low-pressure introductions, and interact through a trust-aware process. The essential functionality is not “chat” by itself, but the structure around connection: neighbourhood grouping, activity-based matching, and safe introduction pathways. Optional features could include badges, interest groups, event reminders, or community announcements, but these should not distract from the core value.

There are also important risks and unknowns. Older adults may have different levels of digital confidence, so the interface must be simple, readable, and forgiving. Location-based features create privacy concerns, so manual suburb or community selection may be safer than precise GPS tracking. Trust is also a major issue: if the app encourages people to meet offline, the design must consider reporting, profile clarity, and gradual introduction rather than instant private messaging.

At this stage, the main design decision is to narrow the project from a general “social app” into a community-first web application focused on local companionship for older adults. This gives the prototype a stronger purpose and helps us judge future features by one question: does this help users form safer, more meaningful local connections?
